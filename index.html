Superfluous details had always been her enemy. As a pragmatic curator of a small urban museum, Elena believed in the power of subtraction. Yet the city’s new digital initiative threatened to exacerbate the very problem it aimed to solve. Ubiquitous screens now bled into every gallery, their glow an ephemeral distraction from the quiet permanence of the artifacts. She was conducting a meticulous inventory of a donation—a collection of 19th-century surgical tools. The brass and steel instruments lay in a velvet-lined case, their purpose brutal but honest. A young intern approached, his face lit by a phone. “We need to move the display case,” he said. “The new interactive kiosk needs the space.” Elena looked at the precarious stack of boxes beside the kiosk. It was a monument to misplaced priorities. “No,” she said, her voice calm but firm. She began to unplug the cables, one by one. The constant hum of progress fell silent. In the hush, the weight of a century felt solid again. The only superfluous thing now was the noise.
