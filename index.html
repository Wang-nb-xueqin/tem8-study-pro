Ubiquitous surveillance cameras watched the plaza. To the pragmatic city planner, they were a necessary tool for order. To the artist, Elias, they were a superfluous layer of steel and glass that exacerbated the city's coldness. He had spent a meticulous week sketching the fountain, its water a ephemeral dance of light. One afternoon, a pigeon, its foot caught in a discarded plastic loop, landed on the fountain's edge. Its struggle was precarious. Elias watched the cameras swivel, their glass eyes recording the bird's distress but offering no intervention. He set down his charcoal. He could not save the world, but he could save this one small, struggling creature. He walked over, his movements slow and deliberate. The bird flinched, but held still. With a careful hand, he snipped the loop. The pigeon, freed, fluttered away into the ephemeral morning light. A small, pragmatic kindness against the city's vast, watchful indifference.
