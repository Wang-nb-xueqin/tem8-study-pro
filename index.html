Precarious had been the nature of his tenure at the consultancy. For three years, Leo had navigated a landscape of quarterly targets and ephemeral alliances, his value measured in billable hours. The ubiquitous hum of open-plan office chatter was a constant, exacerbate only by the ceaseless ping of Slack notifications. A meticulous analyst, he knew the data was often superfluous to the client’s real needs. His pragmatic boss, however, preached that perception was reality. Then the layoffs came. A terse email. A meeting with HR. His badge deactivated. Walking out into the grey afternoon, Leo felt a strange lightness. He had long considered the corporate charade a precarious house of cards. Now, the cards had fallen. He no longer had to sell the ephemeral promise of synergy. The ubiquitous anxiety of performance reviews was gone. In its place was a quiet, pragmatic resolve. He would take his severance, strip away every superfluous subscription, and finally start the independent research project he’d shelved for years. The fall, he realized, had not exacerbated his condition; it had, instead, clarified it.
