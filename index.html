In the cramped offices of the Ministry of Infrastructure, the superfluous paperwork was a silent, choking weed. For decades, a meticulous bureaucracy had ensured every lamp-post required three separate approvals. This did not exacerbate the city’s decay; it was the very engine of it. A pragmatic new director, Elena, saw the rot. She understood that the ephemeral buzz of a new initiative meant nothing against this concrete legacy. The city’s finances were precarious; one bad winter could tip it into insolvency. Yet the ubiquitous forms—pink for sewers, blue for streets—remained untouched. Elena did not rage. She simply removed the wastebaskets. Within a week, the desks were clean. The forms, lacking a final destination, simply vanished. The superfluous had finally met its match: a quiet, pragmatic neglect.
